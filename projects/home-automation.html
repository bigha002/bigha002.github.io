From-Scratch Home Automation

After the astronomical prices of the 2020-2021 global chip shortage began to fall, I bought dozens of ESP32-based microcontrollers of several varieties. At regular market conditions, a controller with 10x the processing power of an Arduino Uno can go for as low as $1.50, not even to mention the addition of wireless connectivity. These cheap chips are perfect for home automation projects!

Thanks to the Covid-19 pandemic and distance learning, every student in the US seems to have an extra webcam collecting dust. Pair a dozen webcams with a dozen wireless computers and what do you get? A dozen cheap security cameras!

I put one camera in every public room of the house (after gaining the consent of my roommates).

Houghton, Michigan, boasts some of the lowest crime rates in the Midwest, making the extensive array of security cameras in my run-down college house practically unnecessary. However, as a hobbyist maker, I've learned that a project doesn't need to be useful to be worthwhile. Besides, it's a fun party trick!

A Raspberry Pi in every room of the house opens the door for some really cool smart-home opportunities. Webcam streaming takes a majority of the computing resources available to a Pi, but there's still plenty left for a simple data collection/transfer routine. Firstly, I attached temperature sensors to every node in the house. Rather than spending another $5 on a premade robust temperature probe, I, in my hubris, decided to make them from scratch.

Making a reliable temperature probe from cheap thermistors is more difficult than I first imagined. Who would've thought that the temperature vs. resistance function of a thermistor is complex and nonlinear! The datasheets for the particular thermistors I bought were curiously absent on the internet, meaning I had to derive my own translation function to calculate temperature. I achieved this by buying an accurate thermometer and using it to observe thermistor resistance at a handful of temperatures around the house. I then put my math minor to use and constructed a nonlinear regression based upon the data, which yielded a continuous function that could accurately translate the ADC value from the thermistor into a usable temperature.

To visualize all of this data, I wrote a python script to generate a live heatmap based on the readings from all temperature probes in the house. This heatmap, along with the security feeds, is pushed periodically to a website hosted on yet another Raspberry Pi in my living room.

Using the temperature sensing platform as a template, I implemented full sensor suites into every node. I now collect air quality data, sound levels, light levels, and IR proximity readings from 9 locations around my house 24/7. I use this data to place plants in optimal growth conditions, minimize sound leakage from our college band, manage heating more intelligently, and show off on my engineering portfolio.
